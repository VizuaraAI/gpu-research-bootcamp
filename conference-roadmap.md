## Recommended Strategy

Your ideal target deadline depends on when you begin the bootcamp and how quickly you ramp up during the research phase.

### If you start in March 2026

- **Immediate stretch goal:** SOSP 2026 abstract (Mar 26) is extremely tight and only realistic if you already have a well-scoped systems idea and preliminary results. For most students, treat this as a forcing function to crystallize your research direction rather than as a hard submission target.
- **Primary target:** NeurIPS 2026 (~May 7). This gives you roughly two months to move from idea to a complete submission. Ambitious but achievable with focused, full-time effort and strong mentorship support during Phase 2.
- **Strong fallback:** ICML 2026 Workshops (Apr–May). Workshop papers are shorter (typically 4–6 pages), have higher acceptance rates, and are an excellent way to get your first peer-reviewed publication and conference experience.

### If you start in April–May 2026

- **Primary target:** NeurIPS 2026 Workshops (Aug–Sept). This timeline lets you complete Phase 1 lectures, find a compelling research question, and write a focused workshop paper.
- **Ambitious target:** ASPLOS 2027 Fall cycle (Sept 9). Suitable if your work has a strong systems or architecture angle.
- **Long-horizon target:** ICLR 2027 or MLSys 2027 (~Oct). These give you a full five to six months of research runway — enough time for a thorough, polished full-paper submission.

### If you start in June 2026 or later

- **Primary targets:** ICLR 2027, MLSys 2027 (~Oct 2026), or ASPLOS 2027 Fall cycle (Sept 9).
- **Backup:** NeurIPS 2026 Workshops (Aug–Sept) may still be reachable if you move quickly.
- **Long-horizon:** Keep an eye on ICML 2027 and NeurIPS 2027 deadlines (typically announced 6–8 months in advance).

### General Advice

1. **Start writing early.** Do not wait until you have "enough" results. Draft your introduction and related work during Phase 1 while the lectures are still fresh.
2. **Aim for one main conference and one workshop.** Submitting a workshop paper in parallel with a full paper gives you a safety net and doubles your chances of getting published.
3. **Use rejections as feedback.** Top venues reject 75–85% of submissions. A rejected NeurIPS paper can be revised and resubmitted to ICLR or MLSys with reviewer feedback incorporated.

## Publication Tiers

Understanding the prestige hierarchy of venues helps you set realistic goals and position your work appropriately.

### Tier 1 — Premier ML Conferences

**NeurIPS / ICML / ICLR**

These are the most competitive and widely recognized venues in machine learning. Acceptance rates typically range from 15–25%. A publication at any of these conferences is a strong signal on a CV and is valued highly in both academia and industry. These venues favor novelty, rigorous experimentation, and broad impact.

### Tier 2 — Top Systems Conferences

**SOSP / OSDI / ASPLOS**

These are the premier venues for systems research, including operating systems, computer architecture, and systems-level ML infrastructure. They are extremely selective (acceptance rates often below 20%) and carry enormous prestige within the systems community. If your research involves GPU kernel design, compiler optimization, or systems-level ML performance work, these are ideal targets.

### Tier 3 — ML Systems

**MLSys**

MLSys sits at the intersection of machine learning and systems, making it a natural fit for bootcamp projects that combine GPU engineering with ML workloads. It is a respected and growing venue with a strong industry audience. Acceptance is competitive but more approachable than Tier 1 or Tier 2 for work that bridges both domains.

### Tier 4 — Workshops

**NeurIPS Workshops / ICML Workshops / ICLR Workshops**

Workshop papers are shorter (4–6 pages), undergo lighter review, and have higher acceptance rates. They are an outstanding entry point for first-time authors. A workshop publication lets you present your work, receive feedback from experts, and build your publication record — all of which strengthen a future full-paper submission to a main conference.

## How the Bootcamp Prepares You

### Phase 1 — Lectures and Foundations

Phase 1 equips you with the technical depth needed to do original research. You will build a strong foundation in GPU architecture, CUDA programming, kernel optimization, and ML systems — the core knowledge required to identify open problems and propose credible solutions. By the end of Phase 1, you should have a clear sense of the research landscape and one or two candidate project ideas.

### Phase 2 — Research and Paper Writing

Phase 2 is where your conference submission takes shape. You will work with 1:1 mentorship to:

- **Scope your research question** — narrowing a broad interest into a concrete, publishable contribution.
- **Design and run experiments** — building benchmarks, collecting results, and iterating on your approach.
- **Write and revise your paper** — drafting each section, incorporating mentor feedback, and polishing the manuscript to meet conference standards.
- **Navigate the submission process** — formatting for the target venue, writing a compelling abstract, and preparing supplementary materials.

The goal of Phase 2 is not just to produce research, but to guide you through the full lifecycle of an academic publication so you can repeat the process independently in the future.
